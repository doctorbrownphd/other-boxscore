## Material Routing

The documented data points and content from the original spec are distributed as follows:

### To Chapter 08: The Salary Ledger

All salary figures, the salary gap visualization, and the performance-to-pay ratio cards belong here. This is the natural home for the economic argument. Specific items:

- Documented salary figures by era ($175 average 1920s, $1,000 star peak 1940s)
- Satchel Paige $80/month ledger entry (Birmingham Black Barons, 1928, Notre Dame RBSC)
- Gibson $1,000/month peak salary documentation
- MLB comparison salary figures (Dickey, Feller, etc.)
- The 1924 World Series share ratio: $307.96 (Monarchs) vs. $5,959.64 (Senators), 19.4 to 1
- Meal money comparison: $1.50/day vs. $15/day
- The five-player performance-to-pay ratio cards (Gibson, Paige, Charleston, Leonard, Bell)
- The salary gap range chart concept
- All Larry Lester salary documentation citations

The 1924 World Series share ratio is a single callout that could anchor Ch 08. It is the sharpest number in this material.

### To Chapter 01: The Color Line

Conditions context that establishes the world the players operated in:

- Stadium rental arrangement (Negro Leagues teams as tenants in MLB-owned facilities)
- Yankees' $100,000 annual revenue from renting to Negro Leagues teams
- The schedule constraint: home games only when the primary tenant wasn't playing

This material strengthens Ch 01's existing world-building about what the barrier looked like in practice.

### To Chapter 02: The Green Book Route

Travel and accommodation conditions that directly connect to the Green Book's purpose:

- Bus travel vs. train travel comparison
- Players sleeping on the bus because no hotel would take them
- The Green Book as infrastructure for survival, not convenience

This material reinforces Ch 02's existing argument about why the Green Book existed.
