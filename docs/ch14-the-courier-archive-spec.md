## One-Line Thesis

The mainstream white press did not cover Negro Leagues baseball. The Black press did, with discipline, commitment, and advocacy journalism that drove the integration of the game. This chapter indexes, analyzes, and makes navigable seventy-five years of that record.

---

## Why This Chapter Exists

Chapter 13 assembled the testimonial record: the players' voices, in audio and transcript, dispersed across institutional archives. This chapter turns to the other half of the existing record that the platform has not yet honored.

The mainstream white press did not cover Negro Leagues baseball in any systematic way. The game as played by Black players, in Black leagues, in Black cities, was largely invisible to the white sports pages. What existed instead was the Black press. The Pittsburgh Courier, the Chicago Defender, the Baltimore Afro-American, the New York Amsterdam News, the Kansas City Call, the Atlanta Daily World, and dozens of regional Black weeklies printed box scores, standings, season previews, player profiles, owner disputes, Eastern Colored League governance arguments, East-West All-Star game coverage, Caribbean winter league dispatches, and the sustained advocacy journalism that pushed baseball toward integration for thirty years before Jackie Robinson arrived.

The Black press did not just cover the Negro Leagues. It argued for them, shaped them, named their heroes, created the vote-in mechanism for the East-West game that preceded the MLB All-Star vote, and then argued for their dissolution by campaigning for integration with the ferocity that Wendell Smith brought to the Pittsburgh Courier and Sam Lacy brought to the Baltimore Afro-American. Both won Cooperstown's J.G. Taylor Spink Award. Both sat on the 1971 Special Committee on Negro Leagues. Neither their journalism nor the journalism of the hundreds of Black writers who covered the game before and alongside them has been assembled as a single searchable corpus.

This chapter assembles it. Seventy-five years, spanning from the formation of the Negro National League in 1920 through the effective end of the Negro American League in 1960 and the integration's aftermath through 1995, across the major Black press outlets. The chapter indexes the journalism corpus, makes it navigable by player, paper, writer, year, and topic, applies NLP to surface patterns the volume makes invisible to a single reader, and presents the three journalists whose work best represents the corpus: Wendell Smith, Sam Lacy, and a third journalist selected through the editorial process.

The chapter's core argument: the Black press is a primary source for Negro Leagues history that the historical literature treats as secondary and the digital humanities have not yet systematically processed. The chapter builds the resource.

## The Hook

**The Negro Leagues would not have existed without the Black press.**

Leslie Heaphy, Kent State University history professor and one of the foremost experts on Black baseball, said it directly in her 2016 Hall of Fame interview: the Negro Leagues would not have existed without the Black press. It is as simple as that.

The Pittsburgh Courier was the most widely read Black newspaper in the United States by the late 1930s. In 1947, it had a circulation of 330,000 and represented a two-million-dollar business. In the 1930s it operated twelve branches and published fourteen editions. It covered the Homestead Grays home games. It ran box scores. Wendell Smith wrote columns arguing for integration, roomed with Jackie Robinson during the 1946 Montreal season, and wrote the last article he would ever publish. He was the Pittsburgh Courier's sports editor. He was the first Black writer to receive the J.G. Taylor Spink Award.

The Chicago Defender, founded in 1905, had already built the national circulation and distribution network that the Courier later imitated. The Baltimore Afro-American was Sam Lacy's paper. Lacy covered the game for the Afro-American for decades, became the first Black member of the Baseball Writers' Association of America, and received his own Spink Award in 1997. He was 99 when he died in 2003.

The mainstream press covered baseball. The Black press covered Black baseball, and advocated for the integration that would end the separate leagues. That journalism is a record. This chapter is the record.

## Corpus Scope and Definition

**The chapter's corpus spans 1920 through 1995.**

Opening year (1920): formation of the Negro National League. The corpus starts here because this is when Black baseball's organizational record and press record become systematic.

Closing year (1995): the chapter extends through the integration aftermath and into the early era of serious retrospective Black baseball journalism. The Pittsburgh Courier's circulation collapse, the Black press's decline as integration opened mainstream papers, and the emergence of retrospective scholarship by Black writers are all part of the record.

**The major publications in scope:**

- Pittsburgh Courier (1920-1975 Negro Leagues-relevant coverage)
- Chicago Defender (1920-1975)
- Baltimore Afro-American (1920-1975)
- New York Amsterdam News (1920-1970)
- Kansas City Call (1920-1965)
- Atlanta Daily World (1928-1970)
- Norfolk Journal and Guide (1920-1960)
- Michigan Chronicle (1936-1960)
- Philadelphia Tribune (1920-1960)
- Cleveland Call and Post (1928-1965)

**The major writers in scope (partial list):**

- Wendell Smith (Pittsburgh Courier, later Chicago American and Chicago Sun-Times)
- Sam Lacy (Washington Tribune, Chicago Defender, Baltimore Afro-American)

## Original Findings (the "oh wow" moments)

### Finding 1 -- The Corpus Index
A searchable index of every identified piece of Negro Leagues baseball journalism in the in-scope publications across the in-scope years. Article title, author, publication, date, page, content summary, player mentions, topic tags, digitization status, access pathway.

The index has not existed before this chapter. The platform produces it. The contribution is the assembled navigability of a seventy-five-year record.

### Finding 2 -- The Coverage Timeline
A year-by-year visualization of Black press baseball coverage volume and topic distribution. When did coverage peak? When did it thin? The East-West game years (1933 onward) produced coverage spikes. Depression years produced coverage drops. Jackie Robinson's 1947 arrival produced a massive spike in baseball coverage across Black press outlets. The years from 1948 through 1960 produced declining Negro Leagues coverage and growing integration-aftermath coverage. The shape of the timeline is itself a finding about the relationship between the Black press and the game it covered.

### Finding 3 -- The Advocacy Arc
A tracked topic analysis of how the Black press's stance on integration evolved across the corpus years. From the early coverage that treated the Negro Leagues as an institution of pride and permanence, to the advocacy journalism of Smith and Lacy arguing for integration, to the post-integration coverage that documented the leagues' decline. The advocacy arc is the story of how the Black press simultaneously built the Negro Leagues and argued for their dissolution.

The chapter traces this arc through specific writers and specific articles, not just aggregate topic modeling. The human editorial work of identifying the key inflection points is the chapter's contribution.

### Finding 4 -- The Three Writers
Featured sections for three writers whose work best represents the corpus: Wendell Smith, Sam Lacy, and a third writer selected through the editorial process. Each featured writer section includes: biographical context, career arc, representative articles (excerpted within copyright limits), editorial framing that lets the writing carry the argument.

The chapter handles the copyright constraint carefully. The Black press publications are copyrighted material. The chapter presents excerpts under fair use principles, clearly attributed, with links or access pathways to full texts where available. Excerpts are brief. The chapter directs readers to source materials for full access.

### Finding 5 -- The Box Score Recovery
A specific quantitative finding the chapter can produce that no chapter has yet made: how many Negro Leagues game box scores were published in the Black press that have not been incorporated into the Seamheads database? The Seamheads team has documented that box score coverage is approximately 75% complete. The chapter cross-references the corpus index against Seamheads coverage documentation to identify specific games documented in the Black press that represent coverage gaps in the integrated statistical record.

The finding feeds back to Chapter 5 and the platform's foundational data infrastructure. The Black press is not just a journalistic record; it is a statistical recovery resource.

## The ML / AI Pipeline

Five model applications. The chapter's ML investment is heavier than Ch 13 because the corpus is larger and more structured. The Black press journalism corpus is longer-form text than oral history transcripts, which allows more robust NLP.

### Model 1 -- OCR Correction and Article Segmentation

**Problem:** Digitized historical newspapers have OCR errors. Black press newspapers from the 1920s through 1950s have higher OCR error rates than contemporary publications because of print quality, font variation, and digitization resolution. The corpus index requires clean text.

**Approach:** A two-stage pipeline. First, a rule-based post-correction layer that applies period-specific vocabulary (player names, team names, league names, era-specific terminology) to flag likely OCR errors for manual correction. Second, a layout-analysis model that segments newspaper pages into articles, identifies article boundaries, and extracts article-level text with associated metadata (column head, byline, date, page).

**Output:** Per article, clean extracted text with OCR-correction flags documented.

**Confidence label:** Every article is flagged with an estimated OCR quality score. Articles with scores below threshold are flagged for manual review before inclusion in the corpus.

**Why this matters:** The rest of the ML pipeline depends on clean text. Garbage in, garbage out applies to every subsequent model.

### Model 2 -- Named Entity Recognition and Player Linking

**Problem:** The corpus contains thousands of player name mentions across seventy-five years of journalism. The platform needs to link these mentions to canonical player IDs (the same IDs used throughout the platform's data infrastructure) for the corpus to be searchable by player.

**Approach:** A fine-tuned NER model. The platform fine-tunes a general NER model on a hand-annotated sample of the corpus. The training data is annotated for four entity types: persons (with baseball role tag), teams, locations, and events (game-level events, transactions, governance events). The fine-tuned model is applied to the full corpus to produce entity annotations.

Player entity mentions are then linked to canonical player IDs using a disambiguation pipeline that uses era, team context, position, and name form to resolve ambiguous mentions.

**Output:** Per article, a list of canonical player ID mentions with position and context.

**Confidence label:** Named entity annotations are flagged with confidence scores. Ambiguous disambiguations are flagged for manual review. The player-linking results are spot-checked against known biographical data.

**Why this matters:** Player linking is the infrastructure for the searchable corpus. A reader who searches for Josh Gibson finds every article in the corpus that mentions him, linked to his canonical platform player ID and his statistical record in the Chapter 10 engine.

### Model 3 -- Topic Modeling and Advocacy Arc Analysis

**Problem:** The corpus contains tens of thousands of articles across seventy-five years. Pattern identification at scale requires computational tools.

**Approach:** Two complementary topic models.

First, a BERTopic model over the full corpus, producing a stable topic structure reviewed and labeled manually (the same approach as Chapter 13, with the same constraint: the model proposes, humans label). The expected topic structure includes: game coverage, standings and statistics, player profiles, owner governance, advocacy for integration, East-West game, Caribbean winter league coverage, integration aftermath and Negro Leagues decline, Hall of Fame coverage, retrospective journalism.

Second, a time-series analysis of topic prevalence. For each topic in the labeled structure, a year-by-year prevalence curve. The advocacy arc finding comes from the prevalence curve for the integration advocacy topic, which is expected to show a specific shape: low in the 1920s, rising through the 1930s, peaking in the early-to-mid 1940s, declining after 1947, re-emerging as retrospective advocacy in the 1960s-1970s.

**Output:** A reviewed topic structure with year-by-year prevalence curves.

**Confidence label:** Topic modeling limitations for historical newspaper text are documented in the methodology. The year-by-year curves are presented with smoothing and uncertainty bands. The chapter explicitly notes that the topic model is an entry point and that human editorial work identifies the key inflection points.

### Model 4 -- Box Score Detection and Seamheads Cross-Reference

**Problem:** Identifying which articles contain game box scores, extracting those box scores in structured form, and cross-referencing against Seamheads coverage to identify coverage gaps.

**Approach:** A layout classification model that identifies newspaper page regions containing box score tables. For identified box score regions, a structured extraction pipeline that parses the box score format (teams, innings, player lines, pitching lines) into tabular form.

The extracted box scores are then cross-referenced against the Seamheads documented game coverage. Games appearing in the Black press corpus but not in the Seamheads database are flagged as coverage gap candidates for further research.

**Output:** A list of candidate box score recovery cases, each with the source article, extracted box score data, and documentation of why it is not currently in the Seamheads database.

**Confidence label:** Box score extraction accuracy is imperfect for historical newspaper formats. Every candidate recovery case is reviewed manually before being presented to the platform's advisory researchers as a potential contribution to the Seamheads database.

**Why this matters:** This is the model that makes the chapter a statistical-recovery resource, not just a journalism resource. The box score recovery finding connects Ch 14 back to the Ch 10 engine and potentially to the Seamheads and Baseball Reference integrated record.

### Model 5 -- Semantic Search Index

**Problem:** The corpus index needs to be searchable by readers who come with specific questions: what did the Courier write about Oscar Charleston in 1935? What did Sam Lacy say about the Red Sox's integration delay? What did Black press writers say about the East-West game crowds?

**Approach:** A semantic search index over the corpus text using sentence-transformers embedding. Identical approach to Chapter 13 Model 1, applied to a much larger corpus. The index allows semantic queries (not just literal string match) and returns ranked article passages with links to source articles.

**Output:** A reader-facing semantic search interface over the corpus.

**Confidence label:** Search results are ranked candidates, not definitive answers. The interface presents ranked results and directs readers to source materials for verification.

## The Visualizations

Five visualizations. The chapter is text-heavy; the visualizations provide structure and navigation.

### Fig 01 -- The Corpus Timeline

**The chapter's opening artifact.**

A two-panel visualization spanning 1920 through 1995.

Upper panel: a stacked area chart showing annual article volume by publication. The reader sees at a glance which papers were publishing the most Negro Leagues baseball coverage in which years, and how coverage volume shifted across the corpus period.

Lower panel: a stacked area chart showing annual article volume by topic (using the Model 3 topic structure). The advocacy arc is visible in the lower panel: the integration advocacy topic rises through the late 1930s and early 1940s, peaks around 1945-1947, then gives way to integration-aftermath and retrospective topics.

**Annotation:** Six events are marked with vertical lines: 1920 (Negro National League formation), 1933 (first East-West game), 1942 (Smith's integration survey of white NL players), 1947 (Robinson's debut), 1960 (effective end of Negro American League), 1971 (first HOF Special Committee).

**Tech:** D3 stacked area chart. Animated reveal (area fills from left). Toggle to switch lower panel between topic and publication decomposition.

**Mobile behavior:** Panels stack vertically. Timeline spans full viewport width. Annotations collapse to labeled dots with tap-to-reveal.

**Oh wow test:** The reader sees the shape of the advocacy arc in the lower panel. The reader understands that the Black press's relationship to the game it covered changed shape over those seventy-five years, and that the shape is documented.

### Fig 02 -- The Corpus Search

**The chapter's primary interactive artifact.**

A reader-facing search interface over the full corpus. The reader types a query, the semantic search returns ranked article passages, each passage shows the publication, date, author, and a link or access pathway to the full article.

Search examples pre-populated in the interface:
- "Josh Gibson's power hitting"
- "East-West game attendance"
- "Red Sox integration"
- "Satchel Paige Mexico"
- "Sam Lacy integration advocacy"

The search interface is designed to be genuinely useful to researchers, not just demonstrative. The chapter invests in search quality.

**Tech:** Semantic search index served as static pre-computed embeddings. Similarity search runs in the browser using WASM-compiled FAISS or equivalent. No server dependency.

**Mobile behavior:** Search input and result list remain usable at 375px. Each result card shows publication, date, author, and excerpt. Tap-to-expand for full passage.

### Fig 03 -- The Three Writers

**Featured journalist sections.**

A dedicated section for each of three featured writers: Wendell Smith, Sam Lacy, and the third writer selected through editorial process. Each section contains:

The excerpts are selected to show each writer's distinctive voice. Smith's advocacy journalism and his Robinson relationship. Lacy's persistence in the press box fight and his Caribbean coverage. The third writer's contribution to a different dimension of the record.

**Critical constraint:** Every excerpt is reviewed by the Testimony Editor role (extended from Chapter 13) and by Elias for copyright compliance. The chapter presents brief excerpts, clearly attributed, within fair use principles. The chapter does not reproduce substantial portions of copyrighted articles. Every excerpt links to the host archive for full-text access.

**Mobile behavior:** Sections stack vertically. Excerpts remain readable.

### Fig 04 -- The Box Score Recovery Candidates

**The statistical finding made visual.**

A table of candidate box score recovery cases: games documented in the Black press corpus that appear to be absent from or incompletely documented in the Seamheads database. Per candidate:

- Game date
- Teams
- Location
- Source article (publication, date, page)
- Seamheads coverage status
- Extracted box score data (where extraction was successful)
- Status (under review / confirmed gap / submitted to Seamheads researchers)

The table is presented as a research workspace, not a finished dataset. The chapter is explicit that these are candidates requiring verification by Negro Leagues historians before any claim of statistical recovery is made.

**Annotation:** A header note explains the Seamheads database's approximately 75% coverage completeness and the chapter's role as a recovery research tool rather than a corrected database.

**Tech:** Pre-computed table with sortable columns. Status field is updatable as verification progresses (built with static storage that the chapter owner can update post-publication).

**Mobile behavior:** Table collapses to card view. Each card shows date, teams, and status.

### Fig 05 -- The Newspaper Map

**The institutional geography.**

A map of the United States showing the major Black press publications in scope, with their home cities marked. Each publication point shows: founded, circulation peak, years of major Negro Leagues coverage, key writers, and a link to the publication's presence in the corpus index.

The map is not just geographic. It shows the national distribution network the Pittsburgh Courier built (twelve branches, fourteen editions by the 1930s) and the city-specific coverage patterns the Kansas City Call and Atlanta Daily World maintained for their local audiences.

**Annotation:** Migration patterns are indicated: the Great Migration routes (1910-1940) that moved Black readers from the South to the Northern and Midwestern cities where these papers circulated.

**Tech:** D3 map with annotated points and migration route overlays. Static.

**Mobile behavior:** Map remains legible at 375px. Publication points are tap-to-expand.

## Open Questions for Project Owner

**Question 1: ProQuest access.**
ProQuest Historical Black Newspapers is the most comprehensive digital archive of the in-scope publications but requires institutional access. The platform needs a sustainable access pathway for corpus construction. Options: (a) academic institution affiliation for research access, (b) Newspapers.com subscription for the available titles, (c) direct partnership or data license with ProQuest. Recommend investigating (c) as a potential platform partnership. Flagging because the access question materially affects corpus completeness.

**Question 2: Seamheads coordination on box score recovery.**
The box score recovery finding (Finding 5 and Fig 04) is the chapter's most consequential data contribution. It should be coordinated with Gary Ashwill and Kevin Johnson before publication. The chapter's recovery protocol document establishes the collaborative posture; the actual outreach is yours. Recommend early coordination so the protocol aligns with what the Seamheads team can absorb. Flagging.

**Question 3: Third featured writer selection.**
Wendell Smith and Sam Lacy are the canonical pair. The third writer requires selection. Candidates: Fay Young (Chicago Defender, the "Through the Years" column, decades of coverage), Joe Bostic (People's Voice, Harlem, the most confrontational integration advocate), Ric Roberts (Pittsburgh Courier, Caribbean coverage specialist), Dan Burley (New York Amsterdam News, Harlem perspective). Recommend Fay Young for the longevity and the distinct Chicago perspective. Flagging.

**Question 4: Copyright posture for excerpts.**
The Black press journalism corpus is copyrighted material. The chapter's fair use posture permits brief excerpts, clearly attributed, with full-text access pathways. The question is how brief. The copyright note document should specify an excerpt length ceiling (recommend 100 words per excerpt as an explicit policy) and the editorial standard for justifying any excerpt. Flagging because the ceiling is the chapter's most important copyright constraint.

**Question 5: Box score recovery scope.**
The box score recovery finding could be a small set of high-confidence candidates or a large set of lower-confidence candidates. The chapter's value to the Seamheads project is higher with a smaller, verified set than with a large unverified list. Recommend scoping to candidates the chapter can verify before publication, with a documented protocol for adding verified candidates post-publication. Flagging.

**Question 6: Corpus timeline endpoint.**
The spec sets 1995 as the corpus closing year. This is a judgment call. Arguments for 1995: covers the full integration aftermath and the emergence of retrospective scholarship. Arguments for 1960: keeps the chapter focused on the Negro Leagues era. Arguments for 1975: the Courier's circulation collapse as the natural endpoint. Recommend 1975 as a compromise that captures the HOF advocacy journalism (1971-1977) without extending into a qualitatively different retrospective period. Flagging because the endpoint affects corpus size.

**Question 7: Testimony Editor role formalization.**
The chapter extends the Testimony Editor role from Chapter 13. By now the role is clearly needed for both chapters and should be formally defined with a charter. Recommend formalizing the role as part of platform-level agent infrastructure before this chapter enters Gate 2. Flagging.
