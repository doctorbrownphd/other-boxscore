## The Shift

Part One ended in the dark. The barrier. The Green Book. The sundown corridor. Three chapters documenting what it cost just to show up.

Part Two begins here. Chapter 04 is the first chapter that is not about what was done to them. It is about what they built.

This is a structural editorial decision. The reader has earned this chapter. They arrive having traveled the routes, passed through the documented danger, and understood the world the players moved through. Now the chapter opens the gate and they walk into Comiskey Park. 51,723 people are already there.

The tone shift is deliberate and must be protected through the build. Chapter 04 is not grief. It is not outrage. It is wonder. The data earns the wonder. The wonder is documented.

## The Hook

**Have you heard of the East-West All-Star Game?**

On August 1, 1943, 51,723 people came to Comiskey Park on Chicago's South Side to watch the Negro Leagues' annual midsummer classic. The MLB All-Star Game that year drew 31,938. The gap was 19,785 people -- a full minor league stadium worth of fans more.

The mainstream press barely noticed.

The Chicago Defender put it on the front page.

---

## The Thesis

The East-West All-Star Game was the largest annual Black sporting event in America for more than a decade. It outdrew the MLB All-Star Game in 1936 and every year from 1942 through 1948. Fans voted by mail through Black newspapers. More than a million ballots were cast. The game was built by the community, for the community, in a country that excluded that community from the official version of the sport.

This chapter documents three things simultaneously:

**The numbers** -- the year-by-year attendance comparison, 1933 to 1948. The years when the amber line was above the white line. The peak in 1943. The documented data that nobody has visualized at this quality before.

**The community** -- who came, what the game meant, how the Black press covered it, and what it represented in a country where Black Americans were systematically excluded from every official institution of American life including the sport they loved and played better than anyone.

**The players** -- the talent on the field. The Hall of Famers. The names that should have been household names in white America and were household names in Black America. What the game looked like from inside the stands on August 1, 1943, documented from contemporary sources.

## The Original Contribution

Two original elements:

**The dual-line attendance comparison:** The year-by-year East-West vs. MLB All-Star attendance, 1933 to 1948, has never been presented as a designed, sourced, shareable data visualization at museum quality. The fact is known to historians. It has never been shown to a general audience in a form that makes the argument in ten seconds. It is simple. It is devastating. It requires no annotation.

**The side-by-side box score:** A designed presentation of two actual box scores from August 1, 1943: the MLB game played that day and the East-West All-Star Game. Same format. Same columns. Same typographic system. Side by side. One was history. One was forgotten. This presentation, at this quality, in this framing, has not been built before.

The attendance chart is the hook. The box score is the closer. Together they make the chapter's argument: the crowd came, and the game they came to watch deserved the same record.

**The attendance figure note:**

Two figures appear in secondary sources for the peak attendance: 51,723 (1943, Comiskey Park) and 50,256 (1941, Comiskey Park). These are two different years. Both are correct. The 1941 figure is the second-highest recorded. The 1943 figure is the record. Oscar verifies both from Lester's primary text before either appears in the chapter.

The SABR Retrosheet cross-reference confirms: 1942 East-West 48,400 vs. MLB 34,178; 1943 East-West 51,723 vs. MLB 31,938; 1944 East-West 46,247 vs. MLB 29,598; 1946 East-West 45,747 vs. MLB 34,908; 1947 East-West 48,112 vs. MLB 41,123.

## The Visualization

### Fig 01 -- The Comparison

**What it shows:** East-West All-Star Game attendance vs. MLB All-Star Game attendance, 1933-1948. A dual-line chart. The year the East-West exceeded MLB. The peak. The gap at its widest.

**Design:**

Two lines. East-West in integration gold (#d4a64a). MLB in archival blue (#6f8aa8). Both on the same axes. Year on X. Attendance on Y.

The years when the gold line is above the blue line are the chapter's argument. Those years are shaded -- a subtle gold fill between the two lines when gold is above blue. 1936. 1942. 1943. 1944. 1946. 1947. 1948.

No annotation on the shaded years. The reader sees the fill and understands. If they do not understand immediately they hover and the exact figures appear: East-West: 51,723. MLB: 31,938. Gap: 19,785.

The 1943 peak is labeled. Not with an annotation bubble -- with a single amber dot slightly larger than the others. The reader's eye finds it without being directed there.

**The deliberate design decision:**

The chart does not include the years after 1948. That story -- the collapse, the integration extraction -- belongs in a later chapter. This chart ends at 1948, at the peak of what the game had built. The reader does not yet see the fall. This chapter is the wonder. The grief comes later.

**Interactivity:**

Hover state on any data point shows: year, East-West attendance, MLB attendance, gap. Click on any East-West data point opens a brief game summary panel: score, location, notable players, one documented crowd detail from the Black press.

The 1943 click opens: score (West 2, East 1), location (Comiskey Park), Satchel Paige's first East-West starting appearance, Josh Gibson's walk in the first inning as the only baserunner Paige allowed, Buck Leonard's ninth-inning home run. And one line from the Chicago Defender's game account, cited with full attribution.

**Mobile:** The chart collapses gracefully to a simplified view at 375px. The two lines remain distinguishable. The 1943 peak remains labeled. The hover state becomes a tap state.

### Fig 02 -- The Ballot

**What it shows:** The fan voting process. A visual document.

**Design:**

A facsimile of the East-West ballot as it appeared in the Chicago Defender -- not a reproduction (copyright) but a designed evocation. The platform's typography. EB Garamond. The player names in the ballot format. A selection indicator showing Oscar Charleston leading with 43,793 votes in 1933.

Alongside the facsimile: the numbers. Over 1 million ballots cast across the 30-year run of the game. Votes tallied through 20 Black newspapers nationwide. Fans mailing in their selections from across the country -- not clicking, mailing, weeks in advance, certain their vote mattered.

**The argument this visualization makes:**

The East-West Game was democratic in a way the MLB All-Star Game was not. MLB All-Star voting was controlled by managers and players for much of its early history. The East-West Game was fan-built from the first ballot. The community created its own institution, ran it on its own terms, and filled Comiskey Park with it.

**One sourced pull quote displayed in Playfair Display beside the ballot:**

*"The East-West All-Star Game was the biggest event in black America except a Joe Louis fight."*
-- Larry Lester, historian, *Black Baseball's National Showcase*

This quote is displayed as a designed typographic element, not as a blockquote. It is attributed precisely. It is the chapter's emotional center.

### Fig 03 -- The Field

**What it shows:** The 1943 East-West roster and their subsequent Hall of Fame status.

**Design:**

A two-column roster display. East team. West team. Each player's name, team, position. Beside each name: an indicator showing whether they were inducted into the Baseball Hall of Fame, and if so, when.

The HOF indicator is a small gold mark beside the name. Players who were inducted appear with the mark and their induction year. Players who were not inducted but whose credentials Oscar identifies as comparable to inducted players appear with a different indicator -- a documented gap marker.

**The finding:**

37 East-West All-Stars were ultimately inducted into the Hall of Fame. The 1943 roster contains some of the most concentrated talent in the game's history. Satchel Paige. Josh Gibson. Buck Leonard. Cool Papa Bell. Players who the official record -- the MLB record -- did not count as major league players until 2020, when MLB reclassified the Negro Leagues retroactively.

The 1943 roster is presented as what it was: a field of Hall of Famers playing in front of 51,723 people whom the official record did not count as an audience for major league baseball.

**The Paige-Gibson note:**

On August 1, 1943, Satchel Paige started for the West. In his first three innings, the only East baserunner was Josh Gibson, who drew a walk. Paige vs. Gibson. The two greatest players in Negro Leagues history, on the same field, in front of 51,723 people. The mainstream press did not cover it. The chapter documents it with contemporary sources and lets the reader hold that fact.

### Fig 04 -- The Press

**What it shows:** The contrast between Black press coverage and mainstream press coverage of the same event.

**Design:**

A split-panel display. Left panel: representative Black press coverage of the East-West Game -- headlines, scale of coverage, front-page placement -- documented from the Chicago Defender and Pittsburgh Courier archives. Right panel: representative mainstream press coverage of the same events -- minimal, buried, or absent.

**The argument:**

The game happened in two Americas simultaneously. In one America it was the biggest event of the summer. In the other it was invisible. The split panel makes this visible as a designed contrast rather than a stated assertion.

**The documented detail:**

The mainstream press's response to the 1933 game is documented: it was largely ignored. The Hall of Fame's own history notes that "the mainstream press pretty much ignored the East-West Game." The Black press treated it as the Game of Games from the first year. That contrast -- same game, two Americas -- is the chapter's editorial argument made visual.

**Oscar's specific review mandate for this section:**

Every headline and coverage claim must be sourced to a specific documented newspaper edition with date. No general characterizations of "the mainstream press ignored it" without a specific example. The specific example can be the absence of coverage in a named paper on a named date -- that absence, documented, is itself evidence.

### Fig 05 -- The Side-by-Side Box Score

**What it shows:** Two box scores from August 1, 1943. Left panel: the MLB game played that day. Right panel: the East-West All-Star Game. Same typographic format. Same column structure. Same platform design language.

**Design:**

The platform's box score format is built in IBM Plex Mono on the dark background. Both box scores use identical formatting: the same column headers (AB, R, H, RBI, etc.), the same line score structure, the same typographic hierarchy. The only visual distinction is the header: one reads the MLB game, one reads East-West All-Star Game, Comiskey Park.

The design argument: these are the same document. One recorded what happened in the white major leagues. One recorded what happened in the Negro Leagues. The official record treated them as different in kind. The platform presents them as different only in label.

**The relationship to Fig 01:**

The dual-line chart is the hook: it shows the crowd came. The side-by-side box score is the closer: it shows the game they came to watch deserved the same format the official record gave the other one. The attendance chart is the chapter's opening argument. The box score is its closing argument.

**What is in the 1943 East-West box score:**

Satchel Paige started for the West. Josh Gibson drew a walk, the only baserunner Paige allowed in three innings. Buck Leonard hit a ninth-inning home run for the East. West won 2-1. The box score contains the statistical record of a game between players who collectively hold multiple MLB all-time records as of 2024.

**Vera's design mandate:** The two panels must be visually identical in format. The reader must be able to scan from one to the other without any visual cue that one is "more official" than the other. The formatting equality is the argument.

**Oscar's review mandate:** The East-West box score must be verified against the Lester primary text and the Retrosheet game account. The MLB game box score must be verified against Retrosheet. Every statistical entry verified before the visualization ships.

**Mobile:** At 375px the panels stack vertically. The format remains identical between them. The reader scrolls from one to the other and sees the same structure.

## The Celebrities Layer

The East-West Game was the social event of the Black calendar in a way the MLB All-Star Game was not. This layer appears in the August 1, 1943 section and is documented from Black press accounts.

**What is documented:**
- Joe Louis was a regular attendee. His presence at specific games is documented in Black press accounts.
- Lena Horne threw out ceremonial first pitches. Documented from press accounts with specific years.
- Count Basie attended. Documented.
- Billie Holiday and Ella Fitzgerald are documented as attendees in specific years.
- The game was described by contemporaries as the Super Bowl, World Series, Oscars, and Grammys simultaneously.

**Oscar's standard for this layer:**
Every named celebrity appearance must be sourced to a specific contemporary account -- a named Black newspaper, a specific date, a named reporter. "Lena Horne attended the East-West Game" without a specific documented source does not ship. "Lena Horne threw out the first pitch at the 1946 East-West Game, as reported by the Chicago Defender on [date]" ships.

The celebrity layer is the chapter's most vulnerable to imprecision. Oscar holds it to the same standard as every other factual claim. If a specific appearance cannot be sourced to a specific contemporary document, it does not appear.

## What This Chapter Does Not Do

**It does not show the collapse.**

The attendance fall after 1948 -- the integration extraction, the attendance plummet from 42,000 to under 25,000 in two years -- belongs in a later chapter where it receives full treatment. Chapter 04 ends at the peak. The chart ends at 1948.

This is a deliberate editorial decision that Ida enforces. The chapter is wonder. Grief comes later. If the build team proposes extending the chart to show the decline, Ida blocks it and the chart stays at 1948.

**It does not argue for integration reversal or nostalgia.**

The chapter documents what was built. It does not argue that segregation was acceptable or that integration was wrong. The chapter exists in 1943. It shows what was true on August 1 of that year. The reader is trusted to hold both truths -- this was extraordinary, and it should never have been necessary -- simultaneously.

**It does not speculate about MLB attendance comparisons as quality arguments.**

The attendance comparison does not mean the East-West Game was "better" than the MLB All-Star Game in any quality sense. It means 51,723 people came. The chapter states the number. It does not overstate what the number proves.

## ML and AI Components

**Minimal for this chapter.** The data story is clean enough without modeling.

**One analytical addition -- the HOF Gap calculation:**

For every player who appeared in the East-West All-Star Game but was never inducted into the Hall of Fame, the chapter compares their documented statistics and career achievements against the inducted threshold -- using the same criteria the Hall applies. Players who meet or exceed that threshold by documented statistics and are not inducted are flagged with the documented gap marker in Fig 03.

This calculation uses the same methodology as the Criteria Compliance Model from the One Long Impersonation platform -- applied here to baseball rather than rock and roll. The output: a ranked list of East-West All-Stars whose documented credentials exceed the HOF threshold and who were never inducted. The list is labeled as model output, not as fact. Oscar reviews the underlying criteria application.

**No AI narrative generation in this chapter.** The contemporary primary sources are strong enough. The August 1, 1943 section is written from documented accounts, not generated. This is the right call for the chapter that opens Part Two -- the first chapter that celebrates rather than documents damage.
