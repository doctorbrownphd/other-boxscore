## One-Line Thesis

The Negro Leagues record is not only statistical. It is also testimonial. This chapter assembles the existing oral history record, makes it accessible and searchable, and lets the players speak in their own voices about the game the statistics cannot fully describe.

---

## Why This Chapter Exists

The platform has spent twelve chapters arguing the statistical case. The arguments have been quantitative because the case has needed to be quantitative: the official record was incomplete, the recognition was lagging, the economic accounting was missing. Chapters 5 through 12 worked through what the numbers can show.

The numbers cannot show everything.

The Negro Leagues record exists in a second form that the platform has not yet honored. The players gave interviews, sat for oral histories, told their stories to researchers and family members and museum curators while there was time. Buck O'Neil sat for hours of recorded interviews. Mamie "Peanut" Johnson gave her story to NPR, to Michelle Green for the book, to historians and curators. The University of Baltimore Negro League Oral History Collection. The SABR Oral History Collection with more than 500 audio files. The State Historical Society of Missouri Kansas City Monarchs Oral History Collection. The Phil Dixon Buck O'Neil tapes from 1985. The Negro Leagues Baseball Museum archives.

These collections exist. They are dispersed across institutions. They are unevenly accessible. They contain testimony the statistics cannot reach.

This chapter is the platform's testimony chapter. It assembles the existing oral history record into a navigable, searchable resource, properly credited to the institutions that collected it, presented with the editorial care testimony requires. The chapter does not record new interviews; that work belongs to oral historians with proper protocols and institutional support. The chapter aggregates, indexes, and presents the work that already exists.

The chapter operates under a posture the statistical chapters do not: voice precedes analysis. The platform's argument cannot displace the speakers. The chapter is built to give the players their own page on a platform that has been quantifying them. The quantification was necessary. It is not sufficient.

## The Hook

**The players knew what they were.**

The statistics arrived late. Seamheads built the database. Baseball Reference incorporated the record. Major League Baseball recognized the Leagues officially in 2024. The platform you are reading was built on those foundations. The numbers exist now in a way they did not exist when the players themselves were alive.

But the players knew. They told us. They sat for interviews when interviews were possible. They wrote autobiographies. They spoke at museum events, on radio programs, in living rooms with microphones running. Buck O'Neil narrated the Negro Leagues segment of Ken Burns's 1994 PBS documentary and told stories no statistic could carry. Mamie "Peanut" Johnson told NPR in 2003 that her rejection by the All-American Girls Professional Baseball League was the best thing that happened to her career, because it put her in the Negro Leagues with Satchel Paige teaching her the curveball.

These recordings exist. They live in archives at the National Baseball Hall of Fame Library, the University of Baltimore Special Collections, the State Historical Society of Missouri, the Negro Leagues Baseball Museum, SABR's Oral History Committee, NPR's archives, and dozens of smaller collections. The recordings are dispersed. They are unevenly accessible. They are not yet a single resource.

This chapter is the resource. The voices of the players, assembled, indexed, and presented as the testimony chapter the platform has not yet built. The statistics matter. The voices matter more, because the voices came first.

## Original Findings (the "oh wow" moments)

### Finding 1 -- The Aggregated Archive
A single navigable index of every accessible Negro Leagues oral history recording across institutional collections. Title, speaker, interviewer, year recorded, host institution, length, accessibility status, content topics, with link or access pathway per recording. The aggregated index has not existed before this chapter. The platform produces it.

The chapter does not host the recordings themselves except where explicit institutional permission has been granted. The platform links to host institutions, attributes properly, and respects the access policies the institutions have set. The contribution is the index, not the holding.

### Finding 2 -- The Topic Map
A topic-tagged exploration of the testimony content. What did the players talk about most often when they talked about their careers? What did they emphasize? What did they downplay? The chapter applies topic modeling to the available transcripts (with explicit acknowledgment of the limitations of computational topic modeling applied to oral testimony) and produces a navigable topic structure that lets readers find testimony on specific subjects: barnstorming logistics, segregation experiences, white-team competition, salary and economics, integration aftermath, women in the league, equipment and play conditions, family life, faith and community.

The topic map is not a substitute for listening to the testimony. It is an entry point that helps readers find the relevant recordings. The chapter consistently directs readers from the topic map back to the source recordings.

### Finding 3 -- The Three Voices
Three featured speakers presented in depth: Buck O'Neil (the canonical voice, who recorded the most extensive testimony of any Negro Leagues figure), Mamie "Peanut" Johnson (the voice of the women's experience and the late-period Negro Leagues), and one third speaker selected through the editorial process described in the open questions below.

Each featured speaker has a dedicated section with a curated selection of testimony excerpts (audio where rights permit, transcripts where they do not), biographical context, and editorial framing that lets the speaker carry the narrative rather than the platform interpreting it.

### Finding 4 -- The Gap Analysis
A documented finding the chapter must make visible: which players are absent from the oral history record. Players who died before oral history collection became systematic. Players who never sat for an interview. Players whose interviews exist only in informal contexts that did not enter institutional archives. The gap is itself testimony: it documents what the record cannot recover and what the platform cannot present.

For each notable absence, a brief note. Josh Gibson, who died in 1947, gave few recorded interviews. The chapter notes the absence and what the historical record knows about him through secondary testimony (his contemporaries who described him in their own interviews). This is the chapter's most editorially careful section: the gap is presented as gap, not filled with speculation or contemporary reconstruction.

### Finding 5 -- The Institutional Map
A map of the collections themselves. Which institutions hold which recordings. Which collections are publicly accessible. Which require credentialed access. Which have been fully digitized and which exist only on physical media. The institutional map is a research aid for future researchers and an honest accounting of the state of the testimonial record.

## The ML / AI Pipeline

This chapter's ML investment is narrower than the statistical chapters and intentionally so. The technology serves the testimony; the testimony does not serve the technology. Three model applications.

### Model 1 -- Transcript Indexing and Search

**Problem:** The aggregated archive contains hundreds of hours of audio and accompanying transcripts. The chapter needs a search interface that lets readers find specific topics, names, places, and events across the corpus.

**Approach:** A semantic search index over the available transcripts. For each transcript, the platform generates embeddings using a general-purpose sentence-embedding model (sentence-transformers or equivalent). Search queries are embedded the same way and matched against the transcript embedding index.

**Output:** A search interface that returns transcript passages relevant to a reader's query, with timestamps that link back to the source audio where available.

**Confidence label:** The search is presented as a research aid, not as definitive testimony retrieval. The interface shows ranked candidates, not single answers, and directs readers to the source recordings for verification.

**Why this and not full-text grep:** Oral testimony rarely uses canonical terminology. A reader searching for "barnstorming" needs to find a passage where the speaker says "we'd play three towns in a day." Semantic embedding handles this; literal string match does not.

### Model 2 -- Topic Modeling

**Problem:** The topic map in Finding 2 requires identifying the major topics the testimony covers. Manual coding of hundreds of hours of transcript is impractical for the chapter timeline.

**Approach:** A BERTopic-based topic modeling pipeline. The pipeline produces a topic structure (likely 15-30 topics in the optimal range) with representative documents per topic and representative terms per topic.

Critical methodological constraint: every topic produced by the model is manually reviewed and renamed by the chapter's editorial process. The model proposes topic clusters; humans assign topic labels. The platform does not present machine-generated topic labels to readers.

**Output:** A reviewed, manually-labeled topic structure with documented mapping from model output to published labels.

**Confidence label:** Topic modeling applied to oral testimony has known limitations. The chapter discusses these limitations in the methodology document. Topics are presented as entry points, not as content analysis.

**Why this matters:** The topic map is the chapter's primary navigation aid for readers who want to explore the testimony by subject rather than by speaker. Without it, the archive is hard to navigate. With it, readers can find what they are looking for.

### Model 3 -- Audio Transcription (where transcripts are absent)

**Problem:** Some recordings in the aggregated archive do not have published transcripts. For the chapter to include them in the topic map and search index, transcripts must exist.

**Approach:** For recordings where the host institution permits and where no published transcript exists, the platform generates transcripts using Whisper (the OpenAI open-source ASR model). Every generated transcript is reviewed for accuracy by a human editor before inclusion. Speaker dialect, era-specific vocabulary, and Negro Leagues-specific terminology are common sources of transcription error and require manual correction.

**Output:** Reviewed, corrected transcripts for previously untranscribed audio.

**Confidence label:** Generated transcripts are flagged as platform-generated and reviewed. Transcripts published by host institutions are flagged as institutional.

**Critical constraint:** Transcript generation occurs only where the host institution has explicitly permitted it. Some institutions retain transcription rights. The chapter respects these constraints absolutely.

**Why this matters:** Without transcripts, the topic map and search index cannot reach significant portions of the audio archive. The transcription work expands the reach of the chapter while respecting institutional rights.

## The Visualizations

This chapter is structurally different from the statistical chapters. The visualizations are designed around audio and text, not data. Five primary artifacts.

### Fig 01 -- The Archive Index

**The chapter's central artifact.**

A searchable, filterable index of every accessible recording in the aggregated archive. Each entry shows:

- Speaker name
- Interviewer or collector
- Recording date or estimated period
- Length
- Host institution
- Access status (open / restricted / by-request)
- Content topic tags
- Link or access pathway

Default sort: by speaker name alphabetically. Readers can filter by topic, by institution, by access status, by era of recording.

The chapter's posture is service: this index is built for researchers and curious readers, and its primary function is to direct them to the source institutions. The platform does not compete with the institutions; the platform aggregates and points outward.

**Tech:** Pre-computed JSON loaded into a virtualized table. URL-shareable filter and search state. Filter and sort controls accessible by keyboard.

**Mobile behavior:** Table collapses to card view at 768px. Each card shows speaker, length, institution, and access status, with tap-to-expand for full record.

**Oh wow test:** The reader sees the scope of the testimonial record assembled in one place for the first time. Hundreds of recordings, dozens of speakers, multiple institutions, fifty-plus years of collection effort, navigable as a single resource.

### Fig 02 -- The Speaker Player

**The audio interface.**

For each recording where audio is accessible and rights permit, the platform provides an audio player with synchronized transcript. The reader can read the transcript while listening, click any sentence to jump to that audio position, and search within the transcript for specific content.

The player is designed to keep the speaker present. The interface emphasizes voice over commentary. Editorial framing is brief; the speaker carries the narrative.

For featured speakers (Finding 3), the player is paired with biographical context and curated excerpt suggestions for readers new to the speaker's testimony.

**Tech:** HTML5 audio with custom transcript synchronization. The synchronization uses standard WebVTT cue format. Transcripts are pre-generated; cue alignment is done at content production time.

**Mobile behavior:** Player remains usable at 375px with transcript below. Tap-to-jump-to-audio-position remains functional on touch.

### Fig 03 -- The Topic Map

**The thematic navigation.**

A visual representation of the topic structure produced by Model 2. Each topic is a node sized by the number of testimony passages that map to it. Edges connect related topics. Reader hover or tap on a topic reveals a list of representative passages with links to source recordings.

The map is intentionally not a precise quantitative artifact. It is an exploration tool that helps readers find content by subject. The chapter says so explicitly in the accompanying text.

Topics expected to surface include: barnstorming logistics, segregation experiences, salary and economics, integration aftermath, white-team competition (the "we beat them" testimony), equipment and conditions, family and home life, faith and community, the East-West game, Cuba and Latin American play, women in the league.

**Tech:** D3 force-directed graph with hover-to-reveal. Static topic structure; dynamic hover behavior.

**Mobile behavior:** Map collapses to a tap-driven list of topics with associated passages. The visual structure is preserved as long as it serves; below 375px the list view takes over.

### Fig 04 -- The Three Voices

**The featured speakers.**

A dedicated section for each of three featured speakers. Each section contains:

- A brief biographical introduction
- A curated set of three to five testimony excerpts with audio (where rights permit) and transcript
- Editorial context for each excerpt (when recorded, who interviewed, why this passage)
- Links to the speaker's full testimony in the archive index
- A closing note that points back to the speaker's wider record

The three-speaker structure is deliberate. The chapter does not attempt comprehensive coverage; it attempts depth on three speakers and breadth via the archive index.

The chapter handles the editorial framing with care. The introductory text is brief. The excerpt selection is reviewed by the editorial process described in the agent roster. The speakers carry the narrative.

**Tech:** Long-form content with embedded audio players and transcripts. Standard editorial layout, no special tooling.

**Mobile behavior:** Sections stack vertically. Audio players remain functional. Transcripts remain readable.

### Fig 05 -- The Gap Acknowledgment

**The chapter's most editorially careful artifact.**

A visualization that documents the absences. For each notable Negro Leagues figure whose oral testimony is missing or sparse, a brief entry: who they were, when they died, what is and is not in the oral history record, and what secondary sources (others' testimony about them, written accounts, scattered interviews if any) exist.

Josh Gibson is the canonical case: died 1947, before systematic oral history collection. Sparse direct testimony; substantial indirect testimony through contemporaries.

The chapter's tone for this section is reverent and reserved. The gaps are presented as gaps, not filled with reconstruction. The chapter acknowledges what cannot be recovered.

**Tech:** Standard editorial layout with linked context. Each entry is brief.

**Mobile behavior:** Standard text layout. No specialized adaptation needed.

## Open Questions for Project Owner

**Question 1: Third featured speaker selection.**
The chapter features Buck O'Neil and Mamie "Peanut" Johnson by clear merit. The third featured speaker requires selection. Candidates include: Connie Johnson (long-career Monarch with substantial recorded testimony), Ernest Burke (University of Baltimore collection), Quincy Trouppe (catcher-manager with substantial Caribbean experience), Monte Irvin (substantial testimony, MLB transition voice), or a voice from outside the U.S. perspective (a Cuban or Mexican-league participant). Recommend Connie Johnson for the recorded-testimony depth and the Monarchs continuity with O'Neil. Flagging.

**Question 2: NLBM coordination.**
The Negro Leagues Baseball Museum is the most important institutional partner for this chapter. The chapter should coordinate with NLBM directly before publication. Bob Kendrick is the NLBM president and has been the public voice for the museum for years. Recommend outreach. The platform's overall posture toward NLBM should be collaborative throughout the back half of the platform; this chapter is the right place to formalize that relationship. Flagging because the outreach is yours.

**Question 3: SABR Oral History Committee coordination.**
The SABR Oral History Committee is the largest single-source collection. The chapter should engage with the committee to confirm linkages, attribution, and any sensitivities around specific recordings. Recommend outreach. Flagging.

**Question 4: Living-speaker protocol.**
Several Negro Leagues figures whose testimony exists are still living (Connie Johnson, Dennis Biddle, others). Living speakers have additional rights and considerations that deceased speakers do not. The chapter must contact every living speaker (or their authorized representative) before including their testimony, even if the recording was made decades ago and is technically in the public domain through prior release. Recommend explicit protocol; living speakers must approve their inclusion in this specific context. Flagging because the protocol affects the timeline.

**Question 5: The testimony ethics document as platform infrastructure.**
The chapter introduces an ethics posture that is likely to apply to Chapter 14 (Black press corpus) and may apply retroactively to elements of Chapters 5 through 12. The testimony ethics document drafted for this chapter may need to be elevated to platform-level infrastructure rather than chapter-specific. Recommend drafting at chapter level for now, then promoting to platform-level documentation during the platform-architecture review the back half requires. Flagging because the implications extend beyond this chapter.

**Question 6: The Testimony Editor role.**
The agent roster extension proposed for this chapter (Testimony Editor) may be needed for Chapter 14 and may be useful for Chapter 11 (Cooperstown) as well, given that chapter's character clause section. The role needs to be formalized with a documented charter, a set of specific gates, and a designated agent instance. Recommend formalization as part of back-half platform infrastructure. Flagging because the role affects the platform's overall review structure.

**Question 7: ASR transcription limits.**
The Whisper-generated transcripts will have errors. Speaker dialect, era-specific vocabulary, and Negro Leagues-specific terminology are common error sources. The chapter's quality depends on transcript accuracy. Manual review is slow and labor-intensive. The question is how many recordings the chapter can reasonably support at the quality required.
