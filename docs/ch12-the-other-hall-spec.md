## One-Line Thesis

Cooperstown is one Hall. The Cuban, Mexican, Caribbean, Dominican, Puerto Rican, and Venezuelan Halls have been inducting the same players, sometimes earlier, sometimes more completely, and the comparison across institutions is a measurement of which institutions have been doing the work.

---

## Why This Chapter Exists

Chapter 11 evaluated Cooperstown against the platform's methodology and produced a slate of candidates the Hall has not yet recognized. This chapter changes the comparison frame. The Negro Leagues players whose recognition Cooperstown lagged or skipped did not exist in an institutional vacuum. They played in the Cuban League. They played in the Mexican League. They wintered in Puerto Rico and the Dominican Republic. They were watched, scouted, and recorded by institutions that have their own halls of fame, with their own induction histories, their own criteria, and their own track records.

The Cuban Hall inducted José Méndez and Cristóbal Torriente decades before Cooperstown did. Martín Dihigo is in five halls. Willard Brown is in the Caribbean Series Hall. The pattern across institutions is documented and unflattering to Cooperstown: when you look at the recognition timeline across six halls of fame, Cooperstown is consistently late, sometimes by a generation, occasionally by more.

This chapter builds the matrix. Every Negro Leagues-era player of established case (those inducted into any of the six halls, plus the Chapter 11 slate candidates) gets a row. Every hall gets a column. Each cell records induction status and induction year. The chapter's findings come from reading the matrix horizontally (which player is honored by which institutions) and vertically (which institution has been honoring how many of the eligible players, and how early).

The framing matters because the standard discussion of Negro Leagues recognition treats Cooperstown as the only Hall that matters. The matrix shows that the other Halls have been operating in parallel for nearly a century, and that the institutional comparison is a meaningful piece of evidence about which Halls have been doing the work and which have been catching up.

This is the chapter where the platform's argument widens from "Cooperstown has been slow" to "Cooperstown has been slow relative to its peers, and the peers are documented." The chapter does not assert that any specific other Hall is better. The chapter assembles the data and lets the comparison speak.

## The Hook

**There are six Halls.**

Cooperstown is one of them. The Cuban Baseball Hall of Fame opened in 1939, the same year five Cuban-American players were inducted to its inaugural class, including José Méndez and Cristóbal Torriente, who would not enter Cooperstown until 2006. The Mexican Baseball Hall of Fame opened in 1939 as well, with the first selection coordinated by sportswriter Alejandro Aguilar Reyes through newspaper coupons. Martín Dihigo, the Cuban-born star who played in all the leagues he could play in, is in five halls of fame: Cooperstown, Cuba, Mexico, Dominican Republic, and Venezuela.

The Caribbean Baseball Hall of Fame opened in 1996, honoring players for their Caribbean Series performance. It includes 118 inductees through 2025 across Cuba, Mexico, Puerto Rico, Venezuela, the Dominican Republic, Panama, and the United States. Willard Brown is in it. Wilmer Fields is in it.

The Cuban Hall languished for more than fifty years after the revolution, then resumed inducting members in 2014. The Mexican Hall built a new $18.3 million building in Monterrey's Fundidora Park in 2019. The Dominican, Puerto Rican, and Venezuelan halls have continued operating throughout, inducting Negro Leagues players whose names Cooperstown was slow to recognize.

This chapter is the matrix. Every player. Every hall. Every induction year. The comparison is the argument.

## Original Findings (the "oh wow" moments)

### Finding 1 -- The Matrix
A complete six-hall induction matrix for every Negro Leagues-era player of established case. The matrix is the foundational artifact: rows are players (including the Chapter 11 slate candidates), columns are halls (Cooperstown, Cuban, Mexican, Caribbean Series, Dominican, Puerto Rican, Venezuelan), cells contain induction year or "not inducted."

The matrix has never been published in this form. The data exists in scattered hall-of-fame records, but the cross-comparison has not been assembled as a single artifact. The chapter produces it.

### Finding 2 -- The Timing Lag
For each player inducted into Cooperstown and at least one other hall, the year-difference between Cooperstown induction and earliest other-hall induction. The pattern is consistent: Cooperstown is the late hall. For some players the lag is decades. Méndez was inducted in Cuba in 1939; in Cooperstown in 2006. The lag is 67 years.

The headline figure: the median Cooperstown lag, the maximum Cooperstown lag, the count of players whose Cooperstown induction lagged another hall by more than 25 years. These are calculable, specific, and damning when laid out as a single table.

### Finding 3 -- The Coverage Gap
For each hall, the count of Negro Leagues-era players inducted who are not yet in Cooperstown. The Caribbean Series Hall has inducted Wilmer Fields. The Cuban Hall has inducted players Cooperstown has never seriously considered. The pattern across halls reveals which Cooperstown candidates have peer-institution endorsement that the Hall has not acted on.

The finding becomes actionable when combined with Chapter 11: candidates on the Chapter 11 slate who are also in one or more peer halls have an additional layer of institutional endorsement. The chapter quantifies this overlap.

### Finding 4 -- The Institutional Mission Comparison
A side-by-side examination of how each hall defines its mission, its eligibility criteria, and its induction process. Cooperstown's Classic Baseball Era Committee considers a ballot every two to three years. The Mexican Hall has annual elections. The Caribbean Series Hall votes annually during the Series. The differences in process produce different patterns of recognition, and the chapter makes those differences visible.

This finding is the chapter's most institutionally honest section. It does not assert that any particular process is better. It documents the differences and lets the reader assess the implications.

### Finding 5 -- The Dihigo Map
Martín Dihigo is in five halls of fame. He is one of the few players whose career was honored across the institutional landscape during his lifetime and after. The chapter maps Dihigo's induction chronology across the five halls as a case study of what cross-institutional recognition can look like when it happens. The Dihigo map is paired with the Cool Papa Bell map (three halls) and the Josh Gibson map (Cooperstown only, despite playing extensively in Cuba, Mexico, and Puerto Rico) to show three trajectories.

The Gibson contrast is the most editorially loaded element of the chapter. Gibson played and starred in multiple Caribbean leagues. He is in Cooperstown. He is not in the Cuban Hall. He is not in the Mexican Hall. He is not in any other hall. The chapter does not assert this is an oversight by those halls. The chapter asks why the cross-institutional recognition pattern for Gibson differs from Dihigo's, and what the difference indicates about each hall's induction criteria.

## The ML / AI Pipeline

This chapter is intentionally less ML-heavy than Chapters 8-11. The chapter's contribution is institutional and comparative rather than methodologically novel. Three model applications.

### Model 1 -- Name Reconciliation

**Problem:** Hall-of-fame records across six institutions use varying name conventions. Cristóbal Torriente in Cooperstown is the same player as Cristóbal Torriente Coureaux in the Cuban Hall. Martín Dihigo in five halls has slight name variations across each. The matrix requires accurate name reconciliation before any comparison can be made.

**Approach:** A name reconciliation pipeline using:
- Fuzzy string matching (Levenshtein-based) for initial candidate identification
- Birth-year and birth-place matching as disambiguation
- Manual verification for every reconciled identity before the player enters the matrix

For the Negro Leagues universe, the reconciliation uses Seamheads canonical player IDs as the master identifier. Players outside the Seamheads universe (some Caribbean-only stars) use newly-assigned canonical IDs documented in the chapter's data files.

**Output:** A reconciled player identity table mapping hall-specific name records to canonical player IDs.

**Confidence label:** Every reconciliation is flagged with confidence (verified / probable / uncertain). Uncertain reconciliations require additional manual verification before the player enters the published matrix.

**Why this matters:** Without name reconciliation, the matrix is unreliable. With it, the matrix is the foundational artifact the rest of the chapter depends on.

### Model 2 -- Induction-Lag Analysis

**Problem:** For players inducted into multiple halls, what is the year-by-year lag between halls?

**Approach:** A simple computation, not a learned model. For each player inducted into Cooperstown and at least one other hall:
- Earliest other-hall induction year
- Cooperstown induction year
- Lag = Cooperstown year minus earliest other-hall year
- Lag bucket: 0-10 years, 11-25 years, 26-50 years, more than 50 years

The same computation runs for every pairwise hall comparison (not just Cooperstown vs. earliest other). The output is a full pairwise lag matrix.

**Output:** Per player, lag values across all hall pairs the player is inducted into.

**Confidence label:** Induction year is treated as a fact when sourced. Players inducted into now-defunct or hard-to-verify hall iterations are flagged with the lower-confidence designation.

**Why this matters:** Finding 2 (the timing lag) requires this computation. The headline figures the chapter publishes come from this pipeline.

### Model 3 -- Cross-Hall Endorsement Score

**Problem:** Chapter 11's slate candidates would benefit from an explicit measurement of which candidates also have peer-institution endorsement. The Hall of Fame consideration process is more likely to advance candidates with established institutional recognition elsewhere.

**Approach:** Per Chapter 11 slate candidate, a count of peer-hall inductions plus a chronology of those inductions. Candidates inducted into the Cuban Hall or Mexican Hall in the 1940s and 1950s have decades-old institutional endorsement that Cooperstown has not yet acted on. The score is descriptive, not predictive.

**Output:** Per Chapter 11 slate candidate, the count and chronology of peer-hall inductions.

**Confidence label:** Descriptive. The score is not used to argue for induction; it is used to document existing institutional recognition.

**Why this matters:** This is the model that connects Chapter 11 to Chapter 12. A reader who has read both chapters can see, for any slate candidate, both the platform's methodological case and the peer-institution endorsement chronology.

## The Visualizations

Five visualizations.

### Fig 01 -- The Matrix

**The chapter's central artifact.**

A heat-map-style matrix. Rows: players (ordered by Cooperstown induction year for inducted Cooperstown players, then by Chapter 11 slate rank for non-Cooperstown candidates). Columns: the six halls. Cells: induction year, color-coded by decade.

The visual pattern of the heat map is the immediate argument. Players inducted into multiple halls across multiple decades produce filled rows. Players inducted into Cooperstown only produce sparse rows. Players inducted into peer halls but not Cooperstown produce rows where Cooperstown is the empty column.

**Annotation:** Specific players are pre-annotated with their cross-hall stories visible on hover or tap. Méndez: 1939 (Cuba), 2006 (Cooperstown), 67-year lag. Dihigo: 1951 (Cuba), 1964 (Mexico), 1977 (Cooperstown), 1989 (Venezuela), 2002 (Dominican Republic).

**Tech:** D3 heat map with hover/tap detail layer. The matrix is responsive: at desktop viewports the full matrix is visible; at tablet, the matrix scrolls horizontally with frozen player-name column; at mobile, the matrix collapses to a per-player card view.

**Mobile behavior:** Card view shows each player as a row with the six halls as inline cells, induction years in compact format. The matrix structure is preserved.

**Oh wow test:** The reader scrolls through the matrix and immediately sees that Cooperstown is the right-side column with the most empty cells among the major halls. The reader sees Méndez at the top (1939 Cuba, 2006 Cooperstown, lag visible). The reader understands without annotation that the comparison across halls produces a specific pattern.

### Fig 02 -- The Lag Distribution

**The timing finding made visual.**

A histogram of Cooperstown induction lag (years between Cooperstown induction and earliest other-hall induction) for the subset of players inducted into both. The distribution is right-skewed: most lags are positive (Cooperstown is later), and some lags are large.

Median lag, mean lag, and the maximum lag (Méndez at 67 years) are annotated explicitly.

A second component shows the same distribution for each pairwise hall comparison. The Cuban-vs-Mexican lag distribution. The Mexican-vs-Caribbean Series lag distribution. The reader can see how each pair of halls relates to the others.

**Annotation:** The maximum lag case (Méndez) is highlighted with the player's name and the two induction years.

**Tech:** D3 histogram with annotated medians. Static.

**Mobile behavior:** Histogram remains visible at all viewport sizes. The pairwise comparison panel collapses to a selector at mobile.

### Fig 03 -- The Three Trajectories

**The case-study comparison.**

Three horizontal timelines, one per featured player:

1. **Martín Dihigo.** Birth, death, career span, five hall inductions plotted along the timeline. The induction marks are spread across his career and posthumous years, showing the multi-hall recognition arc.

2. **Cool Papa Bell.** Birth, death, career span, three hall inductions (Cooperstown 1974, Cuban 1939 (he played there), Mexican 1939 (he played there)). The chapter notes whether Bell's Cuban and Mexican inductions are documented in those halls' records.

3. **Josh Gibson.** Birth, death, career span, one hall induction (Cooperstown 1972). The other five hall columns are visually empty. The chapter notes Gibson's documented play in Cuba, Mexico, Puerto Rico, and the Dominican Republic during his career.

The three trajectories are positioned visually so the reader can compare. Dihigo's timeline is filled with induction marks across the institutional landscape. Bell's is moderate. Gibson's is sparse.

**Annotation:** Each trajectory has a brief narrative note explaining the pattern. The note for Gibson is the chapter's most editorially careful sentence and is reviewed by Ida before merge.

**Tech:** D3 three-row timeline. Static.

**Mobile behavior:** Timelines stack vertically. Each trajectory remains legible at 375px.

### Fig 04 -- The Hall Profile Comparison

**The institutional differences made visible.**

A side-by-side card layout. One card per hall. Each card shows:
- Founding year
- Location
- Total inductees (as of latest year)
- Current induction process (committee composition, ballot frequency, voting threshold)
- Negro Leagues-era player count
- Notable Negro Leagues inductees (top three by chapter relevance)

The chapter does not rank halls. The cards present each hall's profile and let the reader compare.

**Annotation:** Each card has a brief mission statement, sourced to the hall's own documentation where available.

**Tech:** Six cards in a responsive grid. Pre-computed.

**Mobile behavior:** Cards stack vertically. Each card remains complete at 375px.

### Fig 05 -- The Slate Cross-Reference

**The connection to Chapter 11.**

For each Chapter 11 slate candidate, a small visualization showing which peer halls (if any) have inducted the player. The candidate's name, position, Chapter 11 Rate JAWS, and a six-cell row showing induction status across the six halls.

Candidates with peer-hall inductions are emphasized. The chapter's argument: these candidates have institutional endorsement that Cooperstown has not acted on.

**Annotation:** Each row links to the player's Chapter 11 entry for the full case.

**Tech:** Compact ranked list with inline matrix cells. Static.

**Mobile behavior:** List remains the primary view. The matrix cells collapse to a compact indicator at mobile (number of halls inducted into, with tap to expand).

## Open Questions for Project Owner

**Question 1: Hall outreach.**
The chapter would be substantively stronger with at least minimal outreach to the Cuban, Mexican, Caribbean Series halls (Dominican, Puerto Rican, Venezuelan as well if feasible). Outreach goals: confirm inductee lists, request institutional collaboration or comment, acknowledge the chapter's intent. Recommend outreach for the three most prominent halls (Cuban, Mexican, Caribbean Series). Flagging because the outreach is yours.

**Question 2: Spanish-language presentation.**
The chapter's subject matter is fundamentally bilingual. The platform is English-language. The chapter could (a) remain English-only with proper Spanish-language naming throughout, (b) include a Spanish-language summary or supplementary version, (c) consider full Spanish-language translation. Recommend (a) with thoughtful naming throughout and a brief Spanish-language summary linked in the chapter footer. Flagging because the choice has resource implications and signals about platform reach.

**Question 3: Hall of Fame data acquisition.**
For the smaller halls (Venezuelan, Dominican, Puerto Rican country-specific), comprehensive inductee data may not be readily available online. The chapter's matrix is only as complete as the data supports. The question is whether the chapter ships with incomplete data flagged as such, or whether the chapter waits until data acquisition is more complete. Recommend shipping with documented gaps; the chapter's value is the matrix that exists, not the matrix that does not. Flagging.

**Question 4: Gibson framing.**
The Gibson trajectory in Fig 03 is the chapter's most editorially loaded element. The framing options range from "Gibson is in Cooperstown only despite extensive Caribbean play" (factual) to "Gibson's case raises questions about why peer halls have not inducted him" (more pointed) to "Gibson's case is presented for comparison without commentary" (most reserved). Recommend the factual framing with brief note that Gibson played in Cuba, Mexico, Puerto Rico, and the Dominican Republic. Flagging because this is the chapter's most consequential editorial choice.

**Question 5: Living-player living-hall coordination.**
Several halls have active induction processes; the Mexican Hall just inducted its 2026 class. Several Chapter 11 slate candidates with peer-hall endorsement may have additional opportunity for peer-hall induction in the near term. The chapter could note these opportunities or stay silent. Recommend brief acknowledgment where applicable; the chapter's contribution is to surface the cross-institutional landscape, and noting current opportunities is consistent with that contribution. Flagging.

**Question 6: Caribbean Series Hall scope.**
The Caribbean Series Hall is multi-country and honors players specifically for Caribbean Series performance, not lifetime career. Its inclusion in the six-hall matrix is methodologically defensible (it is a Hall, it inducts, its inductions matter) but it operates on different criteria than the other halls. The chapter handles this through Fig 04 (the hall profile comparison) but the matrix itself treats it as equivalent. Worth confirming this is the right treatment. Recommend current spec. Flagging.
