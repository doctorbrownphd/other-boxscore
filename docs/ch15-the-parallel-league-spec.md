## One-Line Thesis

The Negro Leagues were not a parallel to baseball. They were baseball. This chapter does not prove that. The previous fourteen chapters proved it. This chapter says it.

---

## Why This Chapter Exists

The platform has spent fourteen chapters making an argument. The argument has been quantitative, archival, institutional, testimonial, journalistic. It has been made with Cox models and Bayesian uncertainty bands and HTML heat maps and oral history audio players and semantic search indexes over seventy-five years of Black press journalism.

The coda exists because the argument also needs to be made in plain language. Not simpler language than the evidence supports, but simpler language than the machinery of the previous chapters required. The machinery was necessary. The machinery is not the point.

The point is this: for thirty years, from 1920 to 1950, there were two major leagues in American baseball. One of them was recognized as such. One of them was not. The one that was not recognized produced players who now hold multiple MLB records. It produced players who are in five halls of fame and were inducted into the Cuban Hall before Cooperstown got to them by a generation. It produced journalism that argued for its own dissolution because the journalism knew that what it was covering deserved to exist in one league, not two.

The coda does not have a complex ML pipeline. The coda does not have an animated D3 visualization of its central finding. The coda has three things: the argument stated plainly, the evidence cited precisely, and the question the argument raises that the platform cannot answer.

The chapter is the coda. It is also the platform's thesis statement, placed at the end rather than the beginning because the evidence had to come first.

## The Hook

**There were two box scores.**

On August 1, 1943, at Comiskey Park in Chicago, 51,723 people watched the East-West All-Star Game. The Chicago Defender printed a box score. The box score had the same columns as any other box score. The same notation. The same statistical language. The players in it were major league players.

That box score is what the platform is named for. The other box score. The one that was not in the official record until 2020. The one whose players' statistics were not in the MLB record books until Major League Baseball recognized the Negro Leagues as major leagues in 2024.

The platform has spent fourteen chapters arguing that recognition was correct and overdue. The evidence has been assembled. The wages have been calculated. The Hall of Fame candidates have been ranked. The other Halls have been compared. The voices have been collected. The journalism has been indexed.

This chapter is what is left when the machinery is removed.

There were two box scores. They documented the same game.

## The Chapter's Structure

The coda is not organized like the preceding chapters. There is no ML pipeline section. There is no build sequence with fifteen phases. There is no sensitivity dashboard.

The chapter is organized in five short sections. Together they take approximately thirty minutes to read. That is the right length for a coda.

**Section 1 -- What Was Parallel**

A brief account of what the parallel league actually was: its structure, its cities, its economics, its relationship to the communities it served, its relationship to the white major leagues it was excluded from. Not a summary of prior chapters. A fresh statement of the institutional fact.

The section establishes the word "parallel" and immediately complicates it. The parallel league was not running alongside the white major leagues as a lesser version of the same thing. It was running alongside as the same thing under different conditions. The parallel was enforced, not inherent.

**Section 2 -- What the Evidence Shows**

A precise, citation-dense summary of the platform's findings. Not every finding from every chapter. The findings that matter most, stated in the clearest possible language:

- The wage gap, in 2024 dollars, with its confidence interval (Ch 08)
- The integration delay distribution: the median franchise, the maximum franchise, the league-level effect (Ch 09)
- The integrated leaderboard finding: the catcher result, the outfield result, the names at the top (Ch 10, Ch 11)
- The Mendez 67-year lag (Ch 12)
- The advocacy arc shape: the Black press built the leagues and argued for their dissolution (Ch 14)

Five findings. Each stated in one paragraph. Each with precise citation back to the chapter that produced it.

**Section 3 -- What the Voices Said**

A brief anthology of direct testimony from Chapter 13. Five passages. Each is a player or journalist speaking in their own words about what the game was and what it cost. The passages are chosen to carry the argument without the platform needing to annotate them.

Buck O'Neil. Mamie "Peanut" Johnson. Wendell Smith's writing. Two others from the testimony collection.

The section is the only place in the platform where the argument is made entirely in other people's voices. The platform steps back. The speakers carry it.

**Section 4 -- What the Platform Cannot Answer**

The platform's honest accounting of its limits. Three questions the evidence raises that the evidence cannot resolve:

First: The statistical record is approximately 75% complete. The missing 25% contains games that were played, box scores that were printed in the Black press, performances that occurred and were witnessed and recorded and have not yet been recovered. The platform cannot say what the full record would show. It can say the full record exists and that the recovery work is ongoing.

Second: The parallel league ended as a consequence of the integration that was necessary and right. The players who left for MLB after 1947 were players who deserved to be in MLB. The communities and institutions that lost those players deserve acknowledgment. The platform measures what was taken from the players. The platform does not fully measure what was taken from the communities when the leagues dissolved. That accounting is different and harder and belongs to a different kind of scholarship.

Third: The Hall of Fame consideration for the Chapter 11 slate candidates is ongoing. The Classic Baseball Era Committee has not yet acted on the evidence. The platform has made the case. The platform cannot make the decision.

**Section 5 -- The Other Box Score**

A return to the platform's name. The August 1, 1943 East-West box score. Reproduced as the platform is permitted to reproduce it. Stated plainly: this is what the platform is named for. This is what the platform has been building toward. This is the other box score.

The chapter ends on the box score itself, not on the platform's commentary about it. The box score is the argument. The players' names in the box score are the argument. The date is 1943. The record was not official until 2024. That gap is 81 years. The platform has tried to close it.

## Original Findings

The coda produces no new original findings. It cites the findings from Chapters 8 through 14. The coda's contribution is not new evidence. It is the synthesis.

There is one exception: the coda produces a single summary figure that no prior chapter has produced. The aggregate platform finding, stated as a single compound sentence: the number of players in the platform's Hall of Fame slate whose rate JAWS exceeds the position median, the total wage gap in 2024 dollars at the central estimate, and the median franchise integration delay. Three figures, one sentence, all citations to prior chapters.

That sentence is the platform's summary claim. It appears in Section 2. It is the only sentence in the platform that puts all three findings in the same statement.
