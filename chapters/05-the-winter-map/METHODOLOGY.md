## What This Chapter Does

This chapter maps the transnational careers of Negro Leagues players who competed in Caribbean and Latin American winter leagues alongside their US Negro Leagues seasons. It combines season-level data from the Seamheads Negro Leagues Database with Cuban Winter League, Liga Mexicana, Venezuelan League, Puerto Rican Winter League, and Dominican League records to show where these players actually played, year by year, country by country. The chapter profiles four anchor players (Martin Dihigo, Cristobal Torriente, Willie Wells, Ray Dandridge) whose documented careers spanned multiple countries and decades. The finding is direct: the color line was a US problem, not a baseball problem. The same players excluded from the American major leagues were welcomed, counted, and inducted into halls of fame everywhere else baseball was played.

## Data Sources

### Seamheads Negro Leagues Database
- **Source:** Seamheads.com (Gary Ashwill et al.)
- **URL or archive location:** https://www.seamheads.com/NegroLgDatabase/
- **Coverage:** 1900--1948 player-season records for US Negro Leagues and cross-referenced winter league appearances
- **License:** Research use
- **Access date:** 2026-05-24
- **Known limitations:** Winter league cross-references are partial. Not all Cuban, Mexican, or other Latin American seasons appear in Seamheads. Coverage is stronger for well-documented players (Dihigo, Wells, Dandridge) and weaker for lesser-known figures. Some batting averages and pitching records are available; others are listed only as "played" or "pitched."
- **How used in this chapter:** Primary source for all US Negro Leagues season data and for cross-referenced winter league seasons where Seamheads provides them. Each season record includes year, city, team, league, country, and available statistics. Records sourced from Seamheads are labeled Documented.

### Cuban Winter League Records
- **Source:** Multiple sources: Seamheads (partial), Cuban baseball archives (partial), Wikipedia career summaries
- **Coverage:** Cuban Winter League seasons from 1912 through the 1940s
- **License:** Research use / Public domain (historical facts)
- **Access date:** 2026-05-24
- **Known limitations:** Cuban records have documented gaps in the late 1930s. Not all seasons have box score data. Some seasons are confirmed only by secondary sources (Wikipedia career summaries) and are labeled Reported rather than Documented. The Cuban Winter League operated under different statistical conventions than US leagues, and some averages may not be directly comparable.
- **How used in this chapter:** Season-by-season appearances for players in Cuban leagues. Seasons confirmed by Seamheads box score data are labeled Documented. Seasons confirmed only by Wikipedia or other secondary sources are labeled Reported.

### Liga Mexicana (Mexican League) Records
- **Source:** Seamheads cross-referenced records, Wikipedia career summaries
- **Coverage:** Mexican League seasons from 1940 forward (uneven before 1940)
- **License:** Research use / Public domain
- **Access date:** 2026-05-24
- **Known limitations:** Coverage is uneven before 1940. Most Mexican League seasons for the anchor players are well documented in Seamheads with batting averages and pitching records. Some earlier seasons lack detailed statistics.
- **How used in this chapter:** Season-by-season appearances for players in Mexican League teams (Veracruz, Torreon, Mexico City, Nuevo Laredo, Tampico). Most are sourced from Seamheads and labeled Documented.

### Venezuelan League Records
- **Source:** Wikipedia career summaries, Seamheads (limited)
- **Coverage:** Spotty pre-1946
- **License:** Research use / Public domain
- **Access date:** 2026-05-24
- **Known limitations:** Venezuelan League data is sparse for the period covered by this chapter. Seasons are confirmed primarily by Wikipedia career summaries and are labeled Reported. No detailed statistics (batting averages, ERAs) are available for most Venezuelan seasons in the dataset.
- **How used in this chapter:** Season appearances for Dihigo in Venezuela (1932--1934, Concordia). Labeled Reported because the source is Wikipedia without Seamheads box score confirmation.

### Puerto Rican Winter League Records
- **Source:** Wikipedia career summaries, Seamheads (limited)
- **Coverage:** Well documented from 1938 forward, sparse before
- **License:** Research use / Public domain
- **Access date:** 2026-05-24
- **Known limitations:** Puerto Rican appearances in the current dataset are limited to players with documented San Juan seasons. Coverage is better for post-1938 seasons.
- **How used in this chapter:** San Juan appears as a mapped city. Willie Wells is documented as having played in Puerto Rico (noted in his profile as one of five countries). Specific Puerto Rican season records in the current dataset are limited.

### Dominican League Records
- **Source:** Wikipedia, historical accounts of the 1937 Trujillo recruitment campaign
- **Coverage:** The 1937 Dominican season is extensively documented because of the Trujillo recruitment campaign
- **License:** Public domain
- **Access date:** 2026-05-24
- **Known limitations:** Dominican League records outside the 1937 season are sparse for this period. The 1937 season is well documented precisely because of its political context.
- **How used in this chapter:** Ciudad Trujillo appears as a mapped city. Dominican appearances are included in the aggregate density view.

## Data Processing

### Step 1: Player Career Assembly
- **Tool:** Manual compilation from Seamheads, Wikipedia, and Baseball Reference, structured as JSON
- **Input:** Seamheads player pages, Wikipedia career summaries, Baseball Reference Negro Leagues records
- **Output:** `data/players.json` -- 4 player records with 112 total season entries across 6 countries
- **Accuracy / success rate:** Every season entry includes a `source` field (Seamheads or Wikipedia) and a `confidence` field (Documented or Reported). Of 112 total season entries: 97 are sourced from Seamheads and labeled Documented; 15 are sourced from Wikipedia and labeled Reported. All batting averages and ERAs from Seamheads are transcribed directly. Seasons sourced from Wikipedia where only "played" or "pitched" could be confirmed have generic stat entries.
- **Failures and gaps:** Some seasons between documented appearances are unaccounted for. Dihigo has a gap in 1939 (no documented season in any league). Wells has gaps in certain winter seasons. These gaps represent either undocumented play or genuine inactivity, and the chapter does not impute seasons where none are documented.

### Step 2: City Coordinate Mapping
- **Tool:** Manual coordinate mapping to Wikimedia BlankMap-Americas.svg
- **Input:** 22 cities across 6 countries identified from player career data
- **Output:** `data/cities.json` -- 22 city records with SVG x/y coordinates and country codes
- **Coordinate system:** All coordinates are mapped to the BlankMap-Americas.svg coordinate space using the documented affine transformation. Each city is assigned a single point representing the city center, not a specific ballpark or venue. The SVG viewBox is clipped to focus on the relevant geographic area (viewBox="900 -50 1200 900").
- **Accuracy / success rate:** All 22 cities resolved to coordinates. US cities: Chicago, Pittsburgh, Kansas City, Indianapolis, New York, Newark, St. Louis, Detroit, Philadelphia, Baltimore, Memphis, Atlanta, Minneapolis. Cuban cities: Havana. Mexican cities: Mexico City, Veracruz, Torreon, Nuevo Laredo, Tampico. Caribbean cities: San Juan (PR), Caracas (VE), Ciudad Trujillo (DO).
- **Failures and gaps:** Coordinates are approximate city-center positions on the SVG, not precise ballpark locations. For a transnational map at this scale (continental Americas), city-center precision is sufficient. The visual argument does not depend on sub-city precision.

### Step 3: Career Months Estimation
- **Tool:** Manual estimation from career timelines
- **Input:** Season dates for each anchor player (Negro Leagues summer seasons: approximately April--September; winter leagues: approximately October--March)
- **Output:** Months-per-year estimates hardcoded in `index.html` as `monthsPlayed` per player: Dihigo (11), Torriente (10), Wells (11), Dandridge (10). MLB comparison: Ruth (7), Gehrig (6.5), DiMaggio (6).
- **Accuracy / success rate:** These are rounded estimates, not precise measurements. A player with both a summer and winter league season in a given year is counted as playing approximately 10--12 months depending on league schedules and travel time. MLB season duration (approximately late March through early October) is estimated at 6--7 months.
- **Failures and gaps:** Exact start and end dates for winter league seasons vary by year and league. The estimates are labeled Estimated in the methodology and presented as approximate averages across career. The chart does not claim month-level precision.

### Step 4: Hall of Fame Research and Correction
- **Tool:** Manual verification against hall of fame records, SABR biographies, and primary source documentation
- **Input:** Published claims about player HOF inductions
- **Output:** Verified HOF induction data per player in `data/players.json`
- **Accuracy / success rate:** One significant correction applied. Martin Dihigo is widely described in secondary sources as having been inducted into four or five halls of fame. The Venezuelan Baseball Hall of Fame has confirmed he was never officially inducted there. The chapter uses three verified inductions: Cuba (1951), Mexico (1964), USA (1977). This correction is documented in the player's `note` field and in the HOF dot matrix (Fig 04), where Venezuela shows as not inducted.
- **Failures and gaps:** Dominican Republic induction records for these four players could not be independently confirmed from available sources. The HOF dot matrix shows DR as not inducted for all four players.

## Analytical Methods

### Transnational Career Visualization (Fig 01 -- Three Views)

**What it does:**
Presents player career data as an interactive SVG map with three views: Flow Map (animated season-by-season arcs for a single player), Career Geography (static career portrait for a single player), and Aggregate Density (all players summed by city, filtered by date range).

**Why this method:**
The chapter's argument is geographic. The map is the argument. A tabular presentation of career data would contain the same information but would not communicate the transnational scale of these careers. The three views answer three different questions: "Where did this player go next?" (Flow), "What did this career look like as a whole?" (Geography), and "How many player-seasons did each city host?" (Density).

**Inputs:**
4 player records with 112 season entries, 22 city coordinates, country color coding (US: slate #6f8aa8, Cuba: amber #d4a64a, Mexico: moss #8fa05a, Puerto Rico: bronze #b96f4a, Venezuela: oxblood #a14545, Dominican Republic: tan #a08456).

**Parameters:**
- City dot radius in Flow and Career views: base 6--8px plus 2.5--4px per season played in that city
- City dot radius in Density view: base 8px plus proportional scaling up to 50px based on player-season count relative to the maximum city count
- Arc curvature: quadratic bezier with perpendicular offset of 25% of the distance between points
- Timeline scrubber range: per-player, from earliest to latest documented season year

**Outputs:**
Interactive SVG visualization. No computed outputs are stored. The visualization reads directly from `data/players.json` and `data/cities.json` at runtime.

**Uncertainty:**
Each season entry carries a confidence label (Documented or Reported) from the source data. The visualization does not currently display confidence at the per-season level on the map. All four anchor players have overall confidence labels of Documented (Dihigo, Torriente) or Verified (Wells, Dandridge). Individual seasons labeled Reported in the data (15 of 112) are visually indistinguishable from Documented seasons on the map. This is a known limitation.

**Validation:**
Career paths for all four anchor players were cross-referenced against Seamheads player pages, Wikipedia career articles, and SABR biographical entries. The Dihigo HOF induction correction (three halls, not four or five) was validated against Venezuelan Baseball Hall of Fame records.

**Limitations:**
The chapter profiles four players. The aggregate density view sums only those four careers. A complete transnational map of all Negro Leagues players who played winter ball would be substantially larger. The four anchor players were selected by Oscar for source quality, positional range, and career span, not as a statistical sample. The density view shows a curated subset, not a comprehensive census.

### Career Months Comparison (Fig 02)

**What it does:**
Horizontal bar chart comparing average months of professional baseball played per year: four Negro Leagues stars (10--12 months) versus three MLB contemporaries (6--7 months).

**Why this method:**
The bar chart makes the comparison without commentary. The visual gap between the two groups is the finding.

**Inputs:**
Months-per-year estimates for 7 players (4 Negro Leagues, 3 MLB).

**Parameters:**
Maximum scale: 12 months. Bar color: amber for Negro Leagues, slate for MLB.

**Outputs:**
Static bar chart rendered in SVG.

**Uncertainty:**
All months-per-year values are Estimated. They are rounded averages across careers, not precise measurements of playing time. The comparison is directional (Negro Leagues stars played substantially more months per year than MLB stars) rather than precise. A reader should understand the bars as approximate magnitudes, not exact measurements.

**Validation:**
The directional finding (winter league participation roughly doubled the playing season) is well established in the historical literature. The specific month estimates are consistent with documented season dates for the leagues involved.

**Limitations:**
The comparison uses three MLB players selected for name recognition (Ruth, Gehrig, DiMaggio), not as a representative sample. These three played roughly the same number of months as all MLB players (the MLB season length was standardized). The Negro Leagues players were selected for transnational career breadth, which biases their months-played estimates upward relative to Negro Leagues players who did not play winter ball. The chart compares the most transnational Negro Leagues careers against standard MLB careers. It does not claim that all Negro Leagues players played 10--12 months per year.

### Country Coverage Stripes (Fig 03)

**What it does:**
For each anchor player, a horizontal stripe showing which country they played in for each year, color-coded by country.

**Why this method:**
The stripe chart reveals career patterns: which players bounced between countries every year (Dihigo) versus which settled for stretches (Dandridge in Mexico). The visual pattern is the finding.

**Inputs:**
Season-by-season country codes from `data/players.json`.

**Parameters:**
Year range: minimum to maximum year across all four players. Segment width: proportional to year span. Country colors: same as the map.

**Outputs:**
Static chart rendered in SVG.

**Limitations:**
Years without documented seasons in any league appear as gaps in the stripe. These gaps may represent undocumented play, inactivity, or data limitations.

### Hall of Fame Recognition Matrix (Fig 04)

**What it does:**
Dot matrix showing induction status across five national halls of fame (Cuba, Mexico, Venezuela, Dominican Republic, USA) for each anchor player.

**Why this method:**
The dot matrix shows at a glance which countries counted these players before the United States did. Empty dots in the USA column that are filled in other columns make the chapter's argument visually.

**Inputs:**
HOF induction data from `data/players.json`.

**Parameters:**
Five halls shown. Filled dot = inducted. Empty dot = not inducted.

**Outputs:**
HTML grid rendered by JavaScript.

**Uncertainty:**
HOF induction is a factual binary record. The Dihigo Venezuelan correction is applied. No uncertainty on the factual induction status.

**Limitations:**
This is a preview of the full Chapter 12 matrix. Only four players are shown. The complete matrix covering all Negro Leagues players with transnational hall inductions appears in Chapter 12 (The Other Hall).

## Machine Learning Models

This chapter uses no machine learning models. The spec calls for a name resolution model for cross-league entity matching, but the current implementation uses a manually curated dataset of four anchor players with hand-verified season records. Name resolution at scale (for the full transnational player population) is deferred to the platform data layer build.

## AI-Generated Content

This chapter contains no AI-generated narrative content. All text, captions, hook lines, and player notes were written editorially. The player career data was assembled manually from documented sources.

The career months estimates (Fig 02) are editorial estimates based on documented season dates, not outputs of any generative model.

No AI-generated text, summaries, or conclusions appear in this chapter. The primary sources and the map carry the argument.

## Cross-League Comparisons

### Career Months: Negro Leagues Stars vs. MLB Stars (Fig 02)

**Assumption 1:** A "month played" is defined as a month in which the player was on a professional roster and actively competing. Travel time between leagues is not counted but is acknowledged as adding to the total professional commitment.

**Assumption 2:** The MLB season duration (approximately 6--7 months) is representative of all MLB players in the era. This is accurate because the MLB schedule was standardized.

**Assumption 3:** The four Negro Leagues players selected represent the most transnational careers, not the average Negro Leagues career. Many Negro Leagues players did not play winter ball. The comparison is between the most transnational Negro Leagues careers and standard MLB careers, not between average careers in both leagues.

**Precision cost:** The months-per-year estimates are rounded to the nearest 0.5 month. Actual playing time varied by year and by the specific leagues played. The chart shows career averages, not year-by-year precision.

**Calibration:** No calibration needed. The comparison is directional: did players who played winter ball play substantially more months per year than MLB-only players? The answer is unambiguous at the precision available.

**Confidence interval:** Not applicable to rounded estimates. The finding is robust to reasonable variation in the estimates: even if the Negro Leagues estimates are off by a full month, the gap remains large.

## Reproducibility

**Code:** All visualization code is inline in `chapters/05-the-winter-map/index.html` (MIT licensed). No build step. No external dependencies beyond Google Fonts CDN.

**Data:** All pre-computed data is in `chapters/05-the-winter-map/data/` and is CC0 licensed:
- `players.json` -- 4 player records with 112 season entries, source attribution, and confidence labels
- `cities.json` -- 22 city coordinate records mapped to BlankMap-Americas.svg

**Raw data access:**
- Seamheads Negro Leagues Database: https://www.seamheads.com/NegroLgDatabase/ (research use, web access)
- Baseball Reference Negro Leagues: https://www.baseball-reference.com/negro-leagues/ (web access)
- Wikimedia BlankMap-Americas.svg: https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:BlankMap-Americas.svg (Public Domain)
- Lahman Baseball Database: https://www.seanlahman.com/baseball-archive/ (CC0)

**Environment:** Vanilla HTML/CSS/JavaScript. No Python pipeline. No build step. Open `index.html` in a browser.

**Runtime:** Instant. All data is loaded from two small JSON files (players.json: ~20 KB, cities.json: ~2 KB).

To reproduce the visualizations:
```bash
cd chapters/05-the-winter-map/
# Open index.html in a browser. No build step required.
# Data files are committed to data/
```

To verify the data:
1. Open `data/players.json`
2. Each season entry includes `source` (Seamheads or Wikipedia) and `confidence` (Documented or Reported)
3. Cross-reference against the source URLs listed in the file header
4. Verify HOF induction records against baseballhall.org and national hall records
