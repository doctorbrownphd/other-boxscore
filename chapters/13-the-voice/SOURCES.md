# Negro League Oral History Archives
## Open and public-access sources for The Voice

A working sourcing document. All collections below are publicly accessible online. Licensing terms vary by collection. Always verify current rights for any specific clip before incorporating audio into the entry.

---

## Tier 1: Primary archives with direct online audio access

### 1. SABR Oral History Collection
**URL:** https://sabr.org/oralhistory
**Holdings:** 500+ MP3 audio files of interviews with ballplayers including Negro Leagues, Major Leagues, Minor Leagues, and AAGPBL veterans, plus executives, scouts, authors, writers, and broadcasters.
**Format:** Streamable MP3 audio with associated transcripts.
**Provenance:** Conducted by SABR's Oral History Research Committee (founded 1986) and digitized through collaboration with the National Baseball Hall of Fame Library.
**Use terms:** Made available online for research and general listening. **Not for commercial use** without express written consent from SABR. For the project's research and editorial purposes, this is permissible; for any audio playback embedded in the live publication, contact SABR's Jacob Pomrenke or the Oral History Committee.
**Why this matters:** This is the single largest searchable online collection of Negro League player audio. The "Negro Leagues" category filter exists as a dedicated subset of the larger collection.

### 2. National Baseball Hall of Fame PASTIME Archive
**URL:** https://collection.baseballhall.org
**Holdings:** Oral histories, photos, audio, video, and documents. Negro Leagues content explicitly included in ongoing digitization.
**Format:** Direct web access via the PASTIME (Public Archive System To Interact with the Museum Electronically) interface.
**Provenance:** National Baseball Hall of Fame and Museum, Cooperstown, NY. Digitization conducted in partnership with HistoryIT beginning 2015.
**Use terms:** Per the Roy Campanella oral history page: "NBHoF welcomes you to use materials in our collections that are in the public domain and to make fair use of copyrighted materials as defined by intellectual property law and with proper acknowledgement."
**Specific identified Negro League interviews available:**
- Roy Campanella oral history interview, 1987 July 24 (played Baltimore Elite Giants 1937-1942 before Brooklyn Dodgers)
- Satchel Paige oral history interview, 1981 September 13
- Monte Irvin Hall of Fame interview, 2006
- Ongoing additions of Negro Leagues legends including Cool Papa Bell and Oscar Charleston photo materials, with audio releases continuing.

### 3. Negro Leagues Baseball Museum (Kansas City) Oral History Collection
**URL primary:** https://www.nlbm.com
**URL via Google Arts & Culture:** https://artsandculture.google.com/partner/negro-leagues-baseball-museum
**Holdings:** The Kauffman Foundation-funded NLBM Oral History Project. Interview clips from Negro Leagues players and family members.
**Format:** Selected clips available on the NLBM YouTube channel (NLBMUSEUM) and via Google Arts & Culture story pages. Full films and transcripts not yet fully public per the museum's own notes: *"Oral History transcripts and full access to films are coming soon."*
**Provenance:** Negro Leagues Baseball Museum, founded 1990 by former Negro League players including Buck O'Neil. Located in the 18th & Vine historic district, Kansas City, MO.
**Contact for full access:** nlbmarchives@gmail.com or 816-221-1920 (ask for Curatorial Services).
**Use terms:** Films are property of NLBM. Permissions required for republication; access for research purposes appears to be available through direct contact.
**Why this matters:** This is the *most authoritative* primary-source collection for Negro Leagues oral history, conducted by the museum founded by the players themselves. Direct outreach to the museum is the right path for the project's research and licensed use.

### 4. University of Baltimore Negro League Oral History Collection
**URL:** https://archivesspace.ubalt.edu/repositories/2/resources/76
**Holdings:** Eight oral history interviews conducted in 1998. Interviewees include Ernest Burke, Mamie "Peanut" Johnson Goodman, Gordon D. Hopkins, Hubert V. Simmons, plus family members, NLBPA advocates, researchers, and business associates.
**Format:** Digital versions of items available online via the ArchivesSpace interface.
**Provenance:** Conducted by University of Baltimore history students in Professor Jessica Elfenbein's class while working on an exhibit for the Babe Ruth Birthplace and Museum.
**Use terms:** Rights held by University of Baltimore, made publicly available for research, personal, and educational purposes.
**Why this matters:** Mamie "Peanut" Johnson Goodman is one of only three women to play in the men's Negro Leagues. Her oral history is significant.

### 5. Digital Public Library of America Negro League Primary Source Set
**URL:** https://dp.la/primary-source-sets/negro-league-baseball
**Holdings:** A curated primary-source set including oral history excerpts (James "Red" Moore 1978; Ernest Jones 2008), photographs, documents, and contextual materials.
**Format:** Direct web access through DPLA's portal. The Ernest Jones interview is hosted by the University of South Carolina South Caroliniana Library via the South Carolina Digital Library.
**Provenance:** DPLA aggregator. Original sources cited per item.
**Use terms:** DPLA generally publishes items in the public domain or under explicit use licenses. Each item carries its own rights statement; verify per item.

## Tier 2: Adjacent and supplementary archives

### 6. Library of Congress National Jukebox
**URL:** https://www.loc.gov/collections/national-jukebox/
**Relevance:** Pre-1925 recordings now in the public domain. Includes early jazz and blues recordings that contextualize the same era as the early Negro Leagues.
**Use terms:** Public domain per the Music Modernization Act.

### 7. WPA Federal Writers' Project Slave Narratives
**URL:** https://www.loc.gov/collections/slave-narratives-from-the-federal-writers-project-1936-to-1938/
**Relevance:** Public-domain oral histories from the 1930s. Some contain references to early Black baseball in the South.

### 8. Smithsonian Folkways
**URL:** https://folkways.si.edu
**Relevance:** Audio archive with some interviews from the Negro Leagues era, including blues musicians who frequently traveled with or near barnstorming Black baseball teams.

### 9. Hogan Jazz Archive at Tulane University
**URL:** https://library.tulane.edu/about/departments/hogan-jazz-archive
**Relevance:** New Orleans-focused oral history archive. Includes interviews with musicians who knew or played for Black baseball games in New Orleans during the early Negro Leagues era.

### 10. Institute of Jazz Studies at Rutgers University
**URL:** https://www.libraries.rutgers.edu/ijs
**Relevance:** Largest jazz archive in the world. Holds material connected to the same Black cultural circuit that the Negro Leagues operated within.

## Tier 3: Secondary sources with embedded oral history

Published books containing transcribed oral histories.

### 11. John B. Holway, *Voices from the Great Black Baseball Leagues*
**Publisher:** Da Capo Press (1975-1992). 17 Negro Leagues player interviews.

### 12. John B. Holway, *Black Diamonds: Life in the Negro Leagues from the Men Who Lived It*
**Publisher:** Stadium Books (1989). Chet Brewer, Willard Brown, Buck O'Neil, and others.

### 13. Brent P. Kelley, *Voices from the Negro Leagues: Conversations with 52 Baseball Standouts of the Period 1924-1960*
**Publisher:** McFarland (1998).

### 14. Brent P. Kelley, *"I Will Never Forget": Interviews with 39 Former Negro League Players*
**Publisher:** McFarland (2003).

### 15. Brent P. Kelley, *The Negro Leagues Revisited: Conversations with 66 More Baseball Heroes*
**Publisher:** McFarland (2000).

### 16. Art Rust Jr., *"Get That Nigger Off the Field!": An Oral History of Black Ballplayers from the Negro Leagues to the Present*
**Publisher:** Multiple editions since 1976.

### 17. Neil Lanctot, *Negro League Baseball: The Rise and Ruin of a Black Institution*
**Publisher:** University of Pennsylvania Press (2004).
**Internet Archive:** https://archive.org/details/negroleaguebaseb0000lanc

## Notes

**For the research phase:** SABR and PASTIME are the two most important entry points.

**For licensed audio in the live publication:** The Negro Leagues Baseball Museum in Kansas City is the right institutional partner. Direct outreach to nlbmarchives@gmail.com is the recommended path.

**For free use audio:** Pre-1925 LOC National Jukebox recordings are unambiguously public domain. Some UBalt interviews have permissive educational-use terms.

**The editorial principle:** *Let the players speak in their own voices wherever possible.* The oral history archives make this achievable.
